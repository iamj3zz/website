description: |
  AZALAI – Laboratoire Nomade was an EU Culture Programme-funded itinerant collective (2012–2013) coordinated by Fabbrica Europa (Florence) as a nomadic cultural laboratory bringing together 40+ musicians from Africa, the African diaspora, and Europe. The project's name references the ancient Tuareg salt caravan route across the Sahara — a metaphor for the exchange of culture and music across geographic and cultural boundaries. Operating through residencies, workshops, jam sessions, and performances at European festivals, AZALAI functioned as an open ensemble with rotating casts of musicians at each stop. The project carried particular political significance: it coincided with the 2012–2013 Mali conflict that forced the closure of the original Festival au Désert in Timbuktu, making AZALAI's European performances a way of honoring and continuing that spirit of cross-cultural dialogue.

    title: "AZALAI – Laboratoire Nomade: A Caravan of Sound"
    content: |
      The name AZALAI references one of the world's oldest trade routes: the Saharan salt caravan paths traveled by Tuareg and other nomadic peoples for centuries. In reimagining this concept as a "laboratory," the EU Culture Programme initiative transformed the physical caravan into a cultural caravan—a moving ensemble that carried music, memory, and cross-cultural dialogue across Europe.

      The project emerged at a moment of political urgency. In 2012, the Festival au Désert in Timbuktu, Mali—a celebrated beacon of world music and cultural exchange since 2001—ceased operations due to the military conflict in northern Mali. Rather than allowing this spirit of encounter to disappear, AZALAI extended it: bringing the musicians, philosophies, and energies of that festival into Europe, inviting European artists into dialogue with African traditions, and creating new spaces where music could be both a political and artistic act.

      Led by Fabbrica Europa (Florence) and coordinated by Marta Amico, AZALAI operated as an open ensemble. Unlike a fixed band, it assembled different combinations of musicians at each stop—sometimes 8 musicians, sometimes 12, sometimes more. Core ensemble members (Said Tichiti on guembri, Dimitri Grechi Espinoza on saxophone, Kandia Kora on kora and voice, Hilaire Penda on bass, among others) formed the backbone, while local and visiting musicians joined each residency and performance. This rotating structure reflected the project's philosophy: that genuine cultural exchange happens through deep listening, improvisation, and the willingness to learn from one another—regardless of whether you've played together before.

  - type: text
    title: "The 2013 European Tour: Six Stops Across the Continent"
    content: |
      The 2013 tour opened in **Lille, France** (May 8–19) at Wazemmes l'Accordéon, where the ensemble held residency and opened for Malian griot Salif Keita at the Zenith. This performance set the tone: a conversation between ancient traditions and contemporary expression, between the Saharan and the European, between legacy and innovation.

      The caravan moved to **Amsterdam, Netherlands** (June 2 and July 4–7) for workshops and concerts at the Melkweg and the Amsterdam Roots Festival. In this cosmopolitan hub, musicians from across Africa and Europe encountered one another—sharing techniques, rhythms, and stories in the spirit of the original Festival au Désert.

      **Florence, Italy** (July 4–6) brought AZALAI to the heart of Fabbrica Europa's home base for Festival au Désert/presenze d'Africa (4th edition) at Complesso delle Murate. Here, AZALAI musicians performed alongside TADALAT, a Tuareg ensemble from northern Mali, creating an intimate meeting between the caravan and the living traditions it sought to honor. J3ZZ performed as violinist, bridging European classical training with Saharan musical voices.

      The ensemble arrived at **Novi Sad, Serbia** (July 9–14) for Exit Festival, holding residency with the Novi Sad Academy of Music's string quartet—exploring how orchestral and traditional music languages could merge. They performed on the Reggae Stage, signaling how AZALAI's music crossed genre and cultural boundaries.

      **Boechout, Belgium** (July 26–28) hosted a four-day Sfinks Festival residency, culminating in a July 27 concert featuring musicians from Belgium, Gambia, Mali, the Netherlands, Ghana, Ethiopia, and Madagascar—a living testament to the diaspora and exchange that AZALAI embodied.

      The tour concluded at **Sziget Festival** (August 5–11) in Budapest, Hungary—a showcase of the full 12-musician ensemble and the project's vision. From there, AZALAI made a symbolic return to Africa, organizing a conference in Morocco in collaboration with the Festival Taragalte in M'hamid el Ghizlane, on the edge of the Sahara, completing the caravan's philosophical circle.
